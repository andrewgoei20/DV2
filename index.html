How has the Australian Open grown into a global sporting event?

Held in Melbourne, the Australian Open has grown from a historic national championship into one of the most internationally recognised events in tennis. This visual story explores that growth through attendance, champion geography, and the countries that have shaped the tournament's history.

Story context

How the Australian Open became a global event

The Australian Open began as the Australian Championships in 1905 and gradually evolved into a much larger international event. Major turning points included the beginning of the Open era in 1969 and the move to Melbourne Park in 1988.

These milestones show how the event grew from a national championship into one of tennis’s most prestigious and best-attended tournaments.

1905 Australian Championships begins
1969 Open era begins — amateurs and professionals compete together
1988 Move to Melbourne Park
2026 Record crowds reinforce global status

Attendance story

Attendance reflects the tournament’s rising scale

One of the clearest signs of the Australian Open’s growth is the size of its crowds. Attendance rose steadily across the 2010s, was heavily disrupted during the pandemic period, and then rebounded sharply in the years that followed.

By 2026, the tournament had reached record total attendance, showing that the Australian Open is now operating on a much larger scale than before.

2010–2020 Steady long-term growth
2022 Pandemic-affected low point
2026 Record total attendance

Champion geography

Champion success comes from many parts of the world

Although the Australian Open is hosted in Melbourne, its champions do not come from Australia alone. Over time, singles titles have been won by players from a wide range of countries, reflecting the tournament’s place in a global tennis network.

This spread of champion countries helps show that the Australian Open is not only a national event, but also an international sporting competition with global reach.

Australia Strongest historical title record
United States Most important long-term rival
Europe Major source of champion countries

ERA COMPARISON

How dominance changed across eras

Before the Open era, Australia held a much larger share of Australian Open singles titles. In the modern era, success is spread more broadly across countries such as the United States, Serbia, and Switzerland.

This shift shows that the tournament moved away from early local dominance and became a more internationally competitive event over time.

Australia Strongest historical dominance in the pre-Open era
United States Major force in the Open era
Europe More visible in the modern era

DECADE PATTERN

Champion countries shifted across decades

The Australian Open’s early decades were heavily dominated by Australia, especially before the Open era. Over time, however, title-winning success became more distributed, with the United States becoming stronger in the late twentieth century and newer European countries appearing more often in the modern era.

This decade-by-decade heatmap shows that champion success did not stay concentrated in one place. Instead, the tournament gradually reflected a broader and more international competitive field.

Australia Strongest concentration in the early decades
United States Major force from the 1970s through the 2000s
Modern era A broader range of countries appears as champions

PARTICIPATION FLOW

Singles entries flow into Melbourne from around the world

The Australian Open is staged in Melbourne, but its singles draws are built from a broad international field. Countries such as the United States, Australia, France, and Italy contribute large numbers of main-draw entries, while many others are represented by smaller groups of players.

This flow map shifts the focus from champions to participation, showing how the tournament draws talent from across multiple regions rather than relying on one dominant national base.

RANK SHIFTS

Leading countries changed across different eras

The Australian Open was once dominated by Australia and a small group of traditional tennis nations. Over time, however, the ranking of leading title-winning countries shifted as the tournament became more global and competitive.

This bump chart highlights how some countries declined from early dominance, while others such as Serbia and Switzerland rose more strongly in the modern era.

PRIZE MONEY

Prize money growth spread across the whole tournament

The Australian Open’s growth is not only visible in attendance and international participation, but also in its financial scale. Prize money has risen strongly over time, reflecting the tournament’s development into a larger commercial and global event.

These small multiples compare overall prize-pool growth with changes in selected singles payouts, showing that increases were not limited to champions alone but also extended to players in later and earlier rounds.

Total pool Expanded from about A$25.0m in 2011 to A$111.5m in 2026

REGIONAL SHIFTS

Champion dominance moved across regions over time

The Australian Open’s history was not dominated by the same part of the world in every era. Earlier decades were led heavily by Oceania, while the Americas became stronger in the middle periods and Europe emerged as the most prominent region in the modern era.

This radial chart shows how regional contributions to Australian Open singles championships changed across four major eras. Each quadrant represents one period, while colour shows the relative presence of Oceania, the Americas, Europe, and Asia in title-winning success.

Pre-Open Oceania was by far the dominant region
1990s–2000s The Americas remained strong, but Europe expanded
2010s–2026 Europe became the leading source of champions

CONCENTRATION

Champion success became less concentrated over time

For much of Australian Open history, a small group of countries accounted for most singles titles. This meant success was highly concentrated rather than broadly shared across the international field.

These waffle charts show the share of titles won by the top three countries in each era. Although concentration remained high for much of the tournament’s history, the modern era shows a somewhat broader distribution of title success.

Pre-Open The top three countries accounted for almost all titles in the early era
1970s–1980s Concentration remained high, but title success was less tightly grouped than before
2010s–2026 The top three still dominate, but the share is much lower than in earlier eras
